This page showcases the coding challenges I completed as part of the course "PP434: Automated Data Visualization for Policymaking" (2025–26) at LSE.

CC1: Child Poverty in Developing Countries vis-a-vis the UK

Figure 1: Child Poverty in Developing Countries

Figure 1 highlights that in most sub-Saharan African countries, over 50 percent of children live in households with income below the national poverty line. In countries like South Sudan and Zimbabwe, over 70% of the child population is classified as poor.

Figure 2: Child Poverty Rates by Ethnic Groups in the UK

Figure 2 shows how poverty across different ethnic groups in the UK evolved between 2002/03 and the outbreak of the Covid-19 pandemic. While rates for Bangladeshi, Black African, and Pakistani children remained high, there were dramatic reductions during the decade from 2002/03.

CC2: Labour Market Indicators of India

Figure 1: Unemployment Rate in India (15-64 years)

Figure 1 highlights that the unemployment rate in India rose to an all-time high in 2020 in the last 45 years, crossing the 10% mark.

Figure 2: Employment to Population Ratio (15-64 years)

Figure 2 highlights that the Employment to Population Ratio (WPR) in India dipped during 2020 likely due to Covid-19. The WPR of around 47% indicates low utilisation of the country’s labour potential.

CC3: The Poverty Debate in India

The Indian government’s claim that poverty fell to 5.3% relies on a $3/day line unsuitable for an LMIC; the LMIC cut-off shows nearly one-quarter remain poor.

Figure 1: Extreme Poverty Cut-Off

While the reduction in extreme poverty is an achievement, experts argue the 5.3% figure rests on methodological changes (MMRP) that lower estimates. The $3/day line reflects little more than bare survival.

Figure 2: LMIC Cut-Off

India is a lower-middle-income country, and at the World Bank $4.20/day LMIC threshold, its poverty rate remains 23.9%.

CC10: De-trended Regression

Hypothesis: After controlling for income, Indian states differ systematically in HDI outcomes due to policy effectiveness.

Figure 1: HDI vs log GNI per capita (Fitted Line)

Scatter plot of observed HDI against log GNI per capita with the fitted OLS regression line.

Figure 2: Residuals (Actual − Predicted)

Positive residuals indicate over-performance relative to income; negative residuals indicate under-performance.

Method: Supervised machine learning (Linear Regression). View Colab Notebook.
