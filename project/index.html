PP434 Project

High Income → High Human Development? The Structural Prerequisites for India’s 2047 Vision.

An empirical exploration of India’s human development trajectory (1990–2022)

Aim

This project examines India’s human development trajectory in the context of the Government of India’s ambition to achieve high-income status by 2047 under the Vision 2047 agenda.

The project pursues two linked objectives. First, it documents long-run trends in national and state-level HDI along side economic growth. Second, and more substantially, it examines whether Indian states differ systematically in their ability to convert economic resources into human development, focusing on the potential role of social sector spending, institutional capacity, labour-market outcomes, and social structure.

Data

  • National HDI (1990–2023): Sourced from the UNDP HDRO Data Centre.
  • State HDI (1990–2023): Compiled from the Global Data Lab at Radboud University.
  • Per-Capita Social Sector Spending: Estimated by dividing state-level social sector expenditure from RBI State Finances: A Study of Budgets by state population figures from the MoHFW.
  • State Capacity Proxies: Health capacity is proxied using NITI Aayog’s Health Index 2021 (Key Inputs & Processes domain). Education capacity uses NITI Aayog’s School Education Quality Index 2019 (Governance & Processes domain).
  • Workers–Population Ratio: Sourced from the Periodic Labour Force Survey 2022-23.
  • Social Structure Data: Scheduled Caste and Scheduled Tribe population shares from Census of India 2011.

Findings

Chart 1: India’s HDI trajectory since 1990

This interactive time-series tracks India’s national performance across HDI and its constituent indicators. Disaggregating income, health, and education (through the dropdown menu) reveals a structural divergence: while log GNI per capita accelerates sharply from the early 2000s, improvements in life expectancy and schooling progress more gradually. This pattern motivates the project’s core question: is income growth alone sufficient to deliver broad-based human development?

Chart 2: State HDI trajectories

State-level analysis reveals substantial heterogeneity beneath national averages. Animating trajectories from 1990 to 2022 using the year slider shows a pronounced “fanning-out” effect. States such as Kerala and Goa sustain high HDI outcomes over time, while Bihar and Uttar Pradesh remain persistently at lower levels despite income growth. This dispersion indicates that similar economic capacities can coexist with markedly different human development outcomes.

Chart 3: The Income Gradient- HDI vs log(GNI per capita)

A cross-sectional regression of HDI on GNI per capita shows a strong positive association (R2 ≈ 0.85), confirming that income explains a substantial share of variation in HDI acorss states. However, the dispersion around the fitted line is policy-relevant. Manipur and Kerala outperform income-predicted benchmarks, while Gujarat and Andhra Pradesh underperform, underscoring that income is a necessary but insufficient condition for human development. Chart 4: The Efficiency Ranking- Residual HDI (actual − predicted) by state

Residual HDI ranks states by income-adjusted performance. Positive residuals indicate states that convert income into human development more effectively than predicted, while negative residuals reflect under-performance. Kerala, Sikkim, and Manipur consistently register positive residuals, whereas Gujarat and Andhra Pradesh exhibit large negative deviations. This distribution reframes the policy question on conversion effieciency- what factors enable some states to convert income into human development more effectively than others? Chart 5: Do social sector spending priorities explain residual HDI?

Higher per-capita social sector spending is positively associated with income-adjusted HDI and is statistically significant (p ≈ 0.05). However, it explains only 12.6% of cross-state variation. The contrast between Assam and Gujarat which have similar spending levels (₹11,600–₹11,800), but sharply divergent human development outcomes suggests that fiscal effort alone does not gurantee imporved human development. Chart 6: Do better-run states naturally convert income more effectively into human development outcomes?

Using standardized NITI Aayog Health and Education Indices as proxies for state capacity yields little explanatory power (R2 < 0.10). High-capacity states do not systematically outperform their income benchmarks, nor do low-capacity states consistently underperform. This "capacity paradox" suggests that aggregate bureaucratic indices may capture formal inputs and compliance rather than implementation quality or last-mile effectiveness. Chart 7: Does workforce participation explain income-adjusted HDI?

This chart ranks states by their WPR, with bars representing their development efficiency (residual HDI). The visual pattern, or distinct lack of one, is telling. If higher employment drove better development, we would observe a clear concentration of positive residuals among high-WPR states.Instead, the chart shows a near-random distribution of over- and under-performers. High-employment states such as Himachal Pradesh (77%) and Chhattisgarh (73%) display sharply divergent outcomes. With a negligible R2 ≈ 0.01, the evidence suggests that the quantity of employment alone matters far less than its quality in shaping human development outcomes. View Google Colab Notebook

Chart 8: Do social structure and historical disadvantage shape income-adjusted HDI?

A statistically significant negative association (p < 0.05) emerges between SC population share and income-adjusted HDI (see left panel). States with higher SC concentrations are more likely to underperform relative to income benchmarks, indicating that caste stratification functions as a unique "structural drag," impeding the conversion of economic resources into human development. However, no comparable relationship is observed for ST shares. View Google Colab Notebook

Tools

All data cleaning, merging, and analysis were conducted in Python using pandas, NumPy, scikit-learn, and statsmodels. National and state-level HDI series were reshaped into tidy time-series and cross-sectional formats. Income-adjusted HDI performance was estimated using linear regression models of HDI on log GNI per capita, implemented in statsmodels for statistical inference and scikit-learn for prediction and residual construction. Independent variables were standardised (z-scores) using scikit-learn to enable coefficient comparability across models. Visualisations were built in Vega-Lite and embedded via HTML and JavaScript.

A key challenge was non-aligned years across data sources, particularly NITI Aayog’s Health Index (2021) and School Education Quality Index (2019) relative to 2022 HDI estimates. These indices were treated as slow-moving structural proxies, appropriate for cross-state comparison. Union Territories were excluded from spending models due to missing budget data. Social structure data is from Census 2011 as census 2021 has not been conducted yet. All findings are cross-sectional and descriptive, with no causal claims.

Conclusions

This project highlights a central policy risk facing India’s Vision 2047 agenda: rapid income growth may not be sufficient to deliver high human development. While economic capacity explains a large share of variation in HDI across states, substantial differences remain in income-adjusted performance. Higher social sector spending is associated with better outcomes but explains only a limited share of this variation, while commonly used state capacity indicators and aggregate labour participation show little systematic relationship.

By contrast, social structure, particularly the concentration of historically disadvantaged Schedueld castes, emerge as a more consistent correlate of underperformance relative to income benchmarks. Achieving high human development by 2047 will require addressing deeper structural constraints alongside growth.
