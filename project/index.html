PP434 Project

High Income, High Human Development? The Structural Prerequisites for India’s 2047 Vision.

An empirical exploration of India’s human development trajectory (1990–2022)

Aim

This project investigates the structural prerequisites required to ensure India’s trajectory toward a 'High Income' nation by 2047 is matched by 'High Human Development.' Analyzing the period from 1990–2022, it explores the 'structural decoupling' between economic growth and human development outcomes measured through Human Development Index (HDI). By employing a residual analysis framework to measure state-level 'conversion efficiency', the project identifies how deep-seated constraints, specifically social stratification, act as impediments to human development. The goal is to define the structural corrections necessary to align economic capacity with multidimensional well-being.

Data

  • National HDI (1990–2023): National HDI data were sourced from the UNDP HDRO Data Centre and processed into a tidy, consistent time-series format for analysis and visualisation.
  • State HDI (1990–2023): State and Union Territory HDI time-series data are compiled from the Global Data Lab, Radboud University.Global Data Lab.
  • Health & education spending: For each State and Union Territory shares of education and health expendture were compiled from the RBI State Finances: A Study of Budgets of 2023-24.
  • State capacity proxies: State health capacity is proxied using NITI Aayog’s Health Index 2021, sub-domain on Key Inputs and Processes, capturing workforce availability, facility readiness, service delivery infrastructure, and health-system administrative functionality. Education capacity is measured using NITI Aayog’s School Education Quality Index 2019, governance sub-domain, capturing teacher availability, administrative adequacy, accountability, attendance systems, and institutional arrangements supporting learning outcomes. .
  • Workers–Population Ratio: India's Periodic Labour Force Survey 2022-23.
  • Monthly Per Capita Expenditure: India Household Consumption Expenditure Survey 2022-23.

Findings

Chart 1: India’s HDI trajectory since 1990

This interactive time-series tracks India’s national performance across HDI and its constituent indicators. Using the dropdown menu to isolate Income (GNI per capita) versus Health (Life Expectancy) reveals a structural break: while income has followed an exponential trajectory since the early 2000s, gains in health and education remain linear and gradual. This divergence motivates the project’s central inquiry: is income growth alone sufficient to drive human development?

Chart 2: State HDI trajectories

Beneath the national average lies profound inequality. This bubble chart situates each state by its Human Development (X-axis) and Income (Y-axis). Using the Year Slider to animate the trajectory from 1990 to 2022 reveals a "fanning out" effect. While "Leader" states like Kerala and Goa sustain high-equilibrium outcomes, "Laggard" states like Bihar and Uttar Pradesh remain trapped at lower levels. This dispersion illustrates that states with similar incomes can exhibit vastly different levels of human capability.

Chart 3: The Income Gradient- HDI vs log(GNI per capita)

This scatter plot quantifies the relationship between economic capacity and human development. A linear regression of HDI on log GNI per capita yields a strong positive association (R2 ≈ 0.85), indicating that income explains the majority of cross-state variation in HDI outcomes. However, the dispersion around the fitted line is instructive. States such as Manipur and Kerala achieve HDI levels well above their income-predicted benchmarks, while Gujarat and Andhra Pradesh fall substantially below, underscoring that income is a necessary but insufficient condition for translating growth into human development.

Chart 4: The Efficiency Ranking- Residual HDI (actual − predicted) by state

Chart 4 plots Indian states by their "conversion efficiency"- measured as the residual deviation from the income-predicted HDI benchmark. Positive residuals indicate states that convert income into human development more effectively than predicted by the regression model, while negative residuals reflect under-performance relative to income levels. States such as Kerala, Sikkim, and Manipur record positive HDI residuals, indicating outcomes that exceed what would be predicted by GNI per capita alone. Conversely, Gujarat and Andhra Pradesh exhibit the largest negative residuals, highlighting a "growth-development deificit." This distribution sharpens the policy question: what institutional, sectoral, or labour-market factors enable some states to convert income into human development more effectively than others?

Chart 5: Do spending priorities explain residual HDI?

This scatter plot tests the relationship between per-capita social sector expenditure and income-adjusted HDI performance. The association is positive and statistically significant (p ≈ 0.05), yet fiscal intensity explains only 12.6% of cross-state variation. The contrast between Assam and Gujarat is instructive: despite nearly identical per-capita spending (₹11,600–₹11,800), Assam records a positive development surplus (+0.015), while Gujarat exhibits a substantial deficit (−0.035). This suggests that while fiscal effort is necessary, institutional effectiveness conditions how spending translates into human development outcomes.

PC6. Do state capacity proxies explain residual HDI?

This chart examines whether widely used proxies for state capacity in health and education help explain income-adjusted human development performance across Indian states. The analysis relates HDI residuals to standardized scores from NITI Aayog’s Health Index (Key Inputs and Processes sub-domain) and the School Education Quality Index (Governance Processes sub-domain).

Residual HDIi = α + β1·HealthCapacityi + β2·EducationCapacityi + εi

The estimated associations are extremely small and jointly explain less than ten percent of cross-state variation in residual HDI (R² ≈ 0.10). Neither health nor education capacity scores exhibit a substantively meaningful relationship with income-adjusted human development outcomes. This suggests that aggregate capacity indices, while useful for benchmarking, capture only a limited share of the mechanisms through which states translate resources into improved human development outcomes.

PC8. Do social structure and historical disadvantage shape income-adjusted HDI?

Chart 8 examines whether income-adjusted human development performance is patterned by social structure. Using a cross-sectional specification relating residual HDI to standardized Scheduled Caste (SC) and Scheduled Tribe (ST) population shares, the analysis finds a modest but negative association for SC share. In the bivariate model, a one-standard-deviation increase in SC share is associated with a decline of approximately 0.0066 points in residual HDI (β ≈ −0.0066; z ≈ −2.2; p ≈ 0.03; R² ≈ 0.13). States with higher SC concentration—such as Bihar, Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan, and Punjab—are disproportionately clustered below the income-predicted HDI benchmark. These associations are not causal; rather, they reflect the persistence of historical disadvantage and structural exclusion, which place greater demands on state institutions and shape how effectively income and public spending translate into human development outcomes.

Tools & methods

Workflow

  • Python (pandas/sklearn) for cleaning + regressions.
  • Residual approach: predict HDI from log(GNI), then analyse residuals.
  • Standardised betas (z-scores) for comparability across variables.

Challenges

  • Non-aligned years across indices (treated as proxies; noted).
  • Missing UT budget data (UTs dropped for spending models).
  • Cross-sectional associations only (no causal claims).
